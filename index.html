Reading an OTDR Trace: A Step-by-Step Guide

Which Wavelength Report to Use?

▪ Single-Mode (OS2): Use both 1310nm and 1550nm reports. 1310nm is good for overall loss; 1550nm is more sensitive to bends/macrobends (higher loss at longer wavelengths indicates issues). Compare them to detect anomalies.

▪ Multi-Mode (OMx): Use 850nm for primary analysis (higher loss, worst-case). Compare with 1300nm if available to check for mode-field mismatches.

Key Features to Identify (Based on Example 1310nm Report):

The example trace (above) shows a single-mode fiber at 1310nm with events:

1. Fusion Splices (e.g., Event 3 at 2820.93m, 0.078 dB loss)
A small drop with low reflection (0.543 dB). Good splices have <0.1 dB loss. Higher indicates poor fusion.

2. Connectors/Bends (e.g., Event 4 at 4741.90m, 0.078 dB loss)
Reflective spikes (e.g., -31.82 dB). Check for high loss (>0.75 dB per connector per standards).

3. End of Fiber (e.g., Event 8 at 7822.98m, 0.196 dB loss)
Large drop/reflection (> -16.29 dB). Marks the link end; total loss ~1.668 dB.

Tips: Look for unexpected events (e.g., high loss/reflection). Use dual-wavelength to diagnose (e.g., higher loss at 1550nm = bend). Total loss should match your budget calc.

Power Budgets Explanation (25G/50G/100G Focus)

Power budgets are maximum allowable loss (dB) for reliable operation in high-speed Ethernet (e.g., data center interconnects). They come from standards like IEEE 802.3. The app compares your total loss to budgets for 25G, 50G, and 100G, showing Pass/Fail with margins. Examples (approximate; verify for your setup):
- 25G: ~1.8-6 dB (short/long reach).
- 50G: ~1.6-5 dB.
- 100G: ~1.5-5 dB (e.g., 100GBASE-LR4 for OS2).
Budgets vary by reach/fiber—use safety margin for conservatism.
